# THE REMNANT — Narrative Bible

> The story of the world. The story of you. This document defines the player's origin, the overarching narrative, the central mystery, and the thematic architecture that holds everything together.

---

## PROLOGUE — READ ON FIRST LOGIN

*This is the opening text every player sees before character creation. It sets the emotional register for everything that follows.*

---

You don't remember the exact day the world ended. Nobody does.

It wasn't a bomb. It wasn't a war. It wasn't the kind of catastrophe that announces itself with fire and thunder and gives you the dignity of knowing, in the moment, that everything has changed.

It was a cough.

Someone coughed in a hallway in a building that didn't officially exist, in a mountain range in southwestern Colorado, and within six weeks, sixty percent of the human race had stopped being human in any way that mattered.

They called the pathogen CHARON-7. The ferryman. The thing that carries you across.

Most of the infected became the Hollow — stripped down to hunger and reflex, walking through the ruins of their own lives with just enough memory to make them terrible. Your neighbor fumbling with his car keys forever. A teacher still standing at a chalkboard in an empty school, writing the same word over and over. The worst part isn't the violence. The worst part is the recognition. You can still see who they were.

But CHARON-7 had a second act. One in ten thousand didn't become Hollow. They became something else. Something faster, stronger, more aware than they'd ever been — and dependent on human blood to stay that way. People started calling them vampires. They call themselves the Sanguine. They're not wrong about what they are. Neither are we.

That was seven years ago.

The old world is gone. The highways are rivers of weeds. The cities are tombs. The government, the military, the internet, the electrical grid, the supply chains, the systems that made modern life possible — all of it, gone. What's left is us. The Unturned. The ones who didn't get sick, didn't change, didn't become something else's food supply. We built walls. We found guns. We remembered how to grow things and fix things and make do. We built new communities on the bones of the old ones, here in the Four Corners, where the mountains and the desert and the canyons gave us something to put our backs against.

It's not a good life. But it's a life.

You are here because you survived. That's it. That's your credential. You walked, or drove, or crawled, or were carried to this place — the last stretch of the American Southwest where people are still trying to be people. Where settlements trade and fight and worship and argue about what kind of world to build on top of the one that died.

You have nothing. A pack. A knife. Whatever you were wearing when everything fell apart. You have no faction, no reputation, no allegiance. You are no one. In the old world, that was the worst thing you could be. In this one, it might be the best. No one owns you. No one is hunting you.

Not yet.

The road ahead forks in more ways than you can see from here. There are people who want to rebuild. People who want to conquer. People who want to be left alone. People who aren't people anymore but remember being one. And somewhere in the mountains to the north, buried under rock and silence and seven years of secrets, there is a place called the Scar — the place where CHARON-7 was born — and someone, somewhere, believes that what was made there can be unmade.

You don't know if that's true. Nobody does.

But you're going to find out. Because that's what survivors do. They move forward. Even when forward is dark. Even when forward is teeth.

Welcome to The Remnant.

What's left is what matters.

## THE PLAYER'S ORIGIN

### Who You Are

The player is a **newcomer** — someone who has been surviving on their own or in a small group that recently fell apart. They've arrived at the edges of the Four Corners settlements with nothing but the clothes on their back and whatever skills they carried from the old world and the seven hard years since.

The player does **not** start as a hero, a chosen one, or someone with a secret destiny. They start as one more mouth to feed in a world that can barely feed the mouths it has.

**Character creation** lets the player define:

- **Their background** (what they were before the Collapse) — this influences starting skill bonuses
- **How they survived** (alone, in a group, by running, by fighting) — this influences starting disposition and reputation leanings
- **What they lost** — a brief, player-written or selected detail that becomes a personal quest hook

### Why This Matters Mechanically

Starting as nobody is the point. It means every faction reputation is earned, not given. Every item is scavenged, not starting gear. Every relationship is built from zero. The player's trajectory is entirely a function of their choices.

### The Inciting Event

The player doesn't wander into the Four Corners randomly. They were **drawn here by a signal.**

Three weeks before the game begins, a shortwave radio broadcast began repeating on a loop across a wide swath of the Rockies. No one knows who's broadcasting. The signal is distorted, patchy, but the core message is clear:

> *"...Scar site... containment breach... data survives... if you can read, if you can think, if you are still you... come to the Four Corners... the answer is here... repeating..."*

The broadcast has drawn dozens of people from hundreds of miles in every direction. Some are scientists. Some are scavengers. Some are Sanguine. Everyone wants what's at the Scar — and nobody agrees on what should be done with it.

The player is one of the people who heard the signal. Why they followed it is their own business. Maybe they want a cure. Maybe they want power. Maybe they just need a direction.

## THE CENTRAL MYSTERY

### What Really Happened at the Scar

The game's spine is a slow-burn revelation about the true nature of CHARON-7 and the facility where it was created. This mystery unfolds across the entire game and is the engine that drives the overarching narrative forward.

**What the world believes (common knowledge):**
CHARON-7 was a military bioweapon that escaped containment. An accident. Hubris and bad luck. The Scar is the crater where the military bombed the facility after the outbreak to sterilize it. End of story.

**What is actually true (revealed across the game's three acts):**

**Layer 1 — The Accident That Wasn't**
The facility — codenamed MERIDIAN — wasn't just developing a battlefield incapacitant. It was the front end of a black-budget program to create controllable human augmentation. CHARON-7 was the *successful* prototype. The Sanguine mutation wasn't an accident — it was the intended outcome. The Hollow were the failure state. MERIDIAN was trying to build super-soldiers. They succeeded. Then they lost control.

**Layer 2 — The Bombing Was a Cover**
The military didn't bomb MERIDIAN to sterilize it. They bombed it to bury the evidence. The facility is largely intact beneath the blast crater. Its lower levels — the ones that matter — were designed to survive a direct nuclear strike. The data, the samples, the research — all of it is still there. The radio signal is coming from inside.

**Layer 3 — Someone Survived**
Deep inside MERIDIAN, someone — or something — has been alive for seven years. The radio signal isn't automated. Someone is broadcasting it deliberately. Who they are, what they want, and whether they can be trusted is the central question of the game's final act.

**Possible identities of the broadcaster (player discovers through investigation, faction interaction, and exploration):**

| Possibility | Implication | Narrative Weight |
|-------------|------------|-----------------|
| Dr. Elias Vane, MERIDIAN's lead virologist | He has the data to synthesize a cure — or a more targeted weapon | Hope tempered by suspicion |
| An AI system running MERIDIAN's final protocols | The facility is following its last orders: preserve the research at all costs | The machine doesn't care about you |
| A Sanguine elder — one of the first turned, trapped inside | They've been feeding on stored blood for seven years. They want out. | The predator as prisoner |
| A collective — a small group of survivors sealed inside since Day One | They've been running experiments. On themselves. | What do seven years of isolation do to people? |

The game does **not** reveal which one is true until the player reaches the Scar. Different quest paths and faction allegiances surface different evidence pointing to different answers. The player assembles the truth from fragments.

## THE THREE-ACT STRUCTURE

### Act I — Arrival (Levels 1-10)

**Theme: Survival and belonging**

The player arrives in the Four Corners with nothing. Act I is about establishing a foothold: learning to scavenge, navigate, fight, and interact with the factions. The immediate concerns are visceral — food, water, shelter, not dying.

**Narrative beats:**
- Arrive at Crossroads. Get oriented. Learn the factions exist.
- Complete survival-tier tasks to gain initial reputation with one or more factions.
- Hear conflicting rumors about the radio signal and the Scar.
- First encounter with the Hollow (visceral, terrifying, up-close).
- First encounter with a Sanguine (not combat — a conversation, a negotiation, or a moment where you realize what you're talking to).
- Choose a home base — which settlement do you sleep in? This isn't a permanent faction lock, but it shapes early game experience.
- Act I climax: A coordinated Hollow herd pushes through the River Road corridor. The player participates in a settlement defense. They see the factions either cooperate or fail to. They see what happens when the walls don't hold.

**Player emotional arc:** Fear → Competence → First sense of investment in the world

---

### Act II — Allegiance (Levels 11-25)

**Theme: Power, compromise, and the cost of safety**

The player has survived. Now they have to decide what to do with their survival. Act II is the political heart of the game — faction questlines, moral dilemmas, betrayals, and alliances. The radio signal becomes more urgent. Multiple factions are mounting expeditions toward the Scar, and each wants the player's help — on their terms.

**Narrative beats:**
- Deep faction questlines open up. The player sees each faction's strengths and ugliness from the inside.
- The Accord asks the player to negotiate a blood-tithe treaty with the Covenant of Dusk. It would save lives. It would also normalize feeding humans to vampires.
- The Salters ask the player to raid a Sanguine enclave. Straightforward. Violent. Effective. The enclave turns out to contain Lucid Sanguine who are trying to survive on animal blood. Were they a threat? Does it matter?
- The Kindling reveal they've been experimenting with fire-based "purification" — and it works on early-stage Hollow infection. It also kills the patient about a third of the time. Do you help them refine it or shut them down?
- The Reclaimers intercept part of the radio signal and decode a fragment: coordinates to a supply cache outside the Scar. The cache contains MERIDIAN keycards and a partial personnel file. The lead virologist's name is visible: **Dr. Elias Vane.**
- A Sanguine NPC — one the player may have befriended, feared, or fought — reveals that the oldest Sanguine have been having shared dreams about MERIDIAN. They call it "the pull." They believe the facility is calling them home.
- Act II climax: The factions formally split on the Scar question. The Accord wants to send a diplomatic expedition. The Salters want to take it by force. The Kindling want to burn it. The Reclaimers want to study it. The Sanguine factions have their own agendas. The player must choose who they walk with — or try to walk alone.

**Player emotional arc:** Confidence → Complicity → The slow realization that there are no clean hands in this world

---

### Act III — The Scar (Levels 25-40)

**Theme: Truth, and what you do with it**

The journey to and through MERIDIAN. The Scar is the endgame zone — a massive, multi-level underground facility filled with environmental hazards, dormant security systems, Hollow nests, and revelations. This is where the mystery unravels and the player confronts the central question of The Remnant:

**If you could change what happened, should you? And who gets to decide?**

**Narrative beats:**
- The approach to the Scar is a gauntlet. The surface zone is irradiated (or rumored to be — it's actually a chemical deterrent, not radiation). Hollow concentrations are extreme. Sanguine Ferals circle the perimeter.
- Entering MERIDIAN requires either keycards (Reclaimer questline), brute force (Salter questline), Sanguine biometrics (Sanguine questline — the doors respond to CHARON-7 positive blood), or a secret entrance known only to the Kindling's founder, who was a MERIDIAN janitor before the Collapse.
- Inside MERIDIAN, the player finds:
  - Labs full of data about CHARON-7's true purpose
  - Holding cells where early test subjects — death row inmates — were kept. Some cells show signs of long habitation. Some show signs of escape.
  - A functional cold-storage vault containing viable CHARON-7 samples, both the Hollow strain and the Sanguine strain
  - Evidence that MERIDIAN was funded by a consortium, not just the U.S. military — this wasn't one country's program
  - The broadcaster. Whoever — or whatever — they are.

**The Final Choice:**

The broadcaster (identity varies by which evidence the player found and believed) presents the player with a choice. The MERIDIAN facility has the raw materials and data to do one of three things:

**Option A — The Cure (Synthesis)**
Develop and deploy a cure for CHARON-7. This would reverse Hollow infection, potentially returning millions of people to awareness — traumatized, starving, and suddenly conscious of seven years of horror. It would also strip the Sanguine of their abilities, making them human again. The Sanguine do not consent to this. Some would rather die. The infrastructure to manufacture and distribute a cure on a meaningful scale does not exist. It would take years, and the first doses would go to whoever controls them.

**Option B — The Weapon (Destruction)**
Use the Sanguine strain data to create a targeted pathogen that kills only CHARON-7 carriers — Hollow and Sanguine alike. Clean, final, efficient. It would end the Hollow threat permanently and eliminate the Sanguine entirely. It is, functionally, a genocide. The Sanguine are people. The Hollow *were* people. Is it mercy? Is it murder? Who draws that line?

**Option C — The Seal (Preservation)**
Destroy the MERIDIAN facility entirely. Collapse the tunnels. Bury the data, the samples, and the secrets. No cure. No weapon. The world stays as it is — broken, dangerous, alive. Humanity adapts. The Sanguine remain. The Hollow remain. Nobody gets the power to reshape the species. This is the choice that trusts the world to find its own way — and accepts that it might not.

**Option D — The Throne (Control)**
Take MERIDIAN for yourself — or for your faction. Control the data. Control the samples. Become the gatekeeper of humanity's future. This is the power play. Every faction will either serve you or fight you. The player becomes the most important person in the world — and the most dangerous.

**There is no right answer.** Each option has supporters and enemies among the factions. Each has consequences that ripple through the endgame world state. The game explicitly refuses to tell the player which choice was "correct." The world changes. Life goes on. The Remnant endures — in whatever form the player shaped it.

**Player emotional arc:** Determination → Horror (at what MERIDIAN was) → The weight of choosing for others → Aftermath

## THEMATIC PILLARS

These five themes should permeate every quest, every NPC interaction, every room description, and every system in the game. They are the emotional DNA of The Remnant.

### 1. What Makes You Human Isn't Your Biology

The Hollow were human once. The Sanguine still are — maybe. Humanity in The Remnant is not a species classification. It's a set of choices: empathy, restraint, sacrifice, connection. The game should constantly ask the player to define — through action, not dialogue trees — what "human" means to them. A Sanguine who protects a human settlement at personal cost is more "human" than a warlord who enslaves survivors. The game never says this outright. It shows it.

### 2. Scarcity Reveals Character

When there isn't enough, you find out who people really are. The factions of The Remnant aren't good or evil — they're different answers to the question "what do you do when there isn't enough to go around?" The Accord shares and rations. The Salters take and defend. The Drifters move and trade. The Kindling pray and burn. None of them are wrong about everything. None of them are right about everything. The player should feel genuinely torn.

### 3. Memory Is a Burden

The world had iPhones seven years ago. Everyone over the age of twelve remembers the old world. This isn't a medieval fantasy where hardship is generational and normalized — this is a world full of people who remember grocery stores, hospitals, and feeling safe at night. That memory is both a source of strength (we know what's possible) and a source of anguish (we know what we lost). The game should lean into this: found objects from the old world (a child's drawing, a wedding ring, a to-do list on a phone that will never charge again) should carry emotional weight.

But there is a second half to this pillar, and it is just as important: **not everyone remembers.**

Children born after the Collapse — and there are many of them now — have no before. The apocalypse is their baseline. They are competent, pragmatic, and adapted in ways that disturb the adults who are still grieving. A seven-year-old who can identify Hollow by their sounds before she can read is not broken. She is what comes next. The grief for the old world is a thing that will die with the generation that knew it. These children are the living evidence of that fact.

The game should hold both truths simultaneously: the adults haunted by contrast, and the children for whom there is no contrast. The horror of the old world dying is not just in the Hollow. It's in the little girl at Covenant who sleeps fine.

### 4. The Monster Isn't Always What You Think

The most dangerous entity in The Remnant is not the Hollow, not the Sanguine — it's the human being with power and a justification for using it. Warlord Briggs isn't wrong that strength keeps people alive. Castellan Rook isn't wrong that humans are, from a Sanguine perspective, a food source. Deacon Harrow isn't wrong that fire kills the infected. The game should make the player uncomfortable with how reasonable the worst people sound. The real monsters are the ones who stop questioning themselves.

### 5. Hope Is an Act of Defiance

The Remnant is dark, but it is not nihilistic. Children are born. Gardens are planted. People fall in love in the shadow of the apocalypse. A musician plays guitar by a campfire and for three minutes, nothing is trying to kill anyone. The game should earn these moments by placing them carefully — after horror, after loss, after hard choices. Hope in The Remnant isn't naïve. It's stubborn. It's the decision to plant a tree you might not live to sit under. The game should make the player want to protect these moments, because they are rare, and they are real.

## NARRATIVE THREADS — SIDE STORIES

These are recurring narrative threads that weave through the game alongside the main Scar quest. They are not optional in the sense of "skippable" — they are the stories that make the world feel inhabited.

### The Tithe

The Covenant of Dusk's blood-tithe system: humans voluntarily donate blood in exchange for Sanguine protection. It works. It's also a step toward farming. Where is the line? This thread follows a specific human family who tithes and a specific Sanguine who feeds, and watches what happens to both of them over time.

### The Kindling's Fire

Deacon Harrow's purification experiments. He can burn the early infection out of someone — sometimes. The success rate is climbing. So is his body count. Is he a healer or a zealot? This thread lets the player investigate, assist, or sabotage his work and watch the consequences.

### The Last Doctor

Dr. Ama Osei, the Sanguine virologist trying to find a cure while surviving on animal blood that barely sustains her. She's brilliant, desperate, and deteriorating. Helping her requires bringing her supplies, protecting her from Red Court hunters, and eventually making a choice: does she get the MERIDIAN data, and if so, what will she do with it?

### The Dog

A stray dog appears in the game early on — the one from the River Road room. If the player feeds it, it follows. If the player is kind to it consistently, it becomes a companion. If the player is cruel, it disappears. The dog has no stats, no combat utility, no gameplay function. It is purely a measure of who the player is when nothing is watching. Late in the game, in a moment of genuine danger, the dog does something that matters. What it does depends on how the player treated it.

### Letters Home

Scattered throughout the world are handwritten letters — never sent, never received. Letters from parents to children. From soldiers to partners. From scientists at MERIDIAN to people who'll never read the words. These are not quest items. They have no mechanical value. They exist purely to remind the player that the Hollow shuffling down the highway used to be someone who wrote love letters in the margins of notebooks. Collecting them is its own quiet purpose.

### The Broadcaster

Even before reaching the Scar, the player can tune into fragments of the radio signal from different locations. Each location yields a different snippet. Assembled in order, they tell a story — but the story changes depending on which fragments you find first. This is the game's unreliable narrator mechanic: the truth shifts depending on your angle of approach.

### The Echo

This is the game's most quietly devastating thread. It doesn't announce itself. It accumulates.

The Hollow retain motor memory — fragments of the people they were. A mechanic fumbles with wrenches. A soldier shoulders a rifle it can't aim. Most of the time, this is a detail, an atmosphere beat, a reminder that what you're fighting used to be someone. The Echo is what happens when it stops being atmospheric and becomes personal.

**The hook:** In Act II, the player encounters a specific Hollow in a specific location — the place where their character died in a previous cycle (or, for Cycle 1 players, a location seeded by their personal loss). The Hollow is performing a repetitive action that mirrors something the player character did or would do. It's wearing something recognizable: not a name tag, not a dramatic reveal, just a detail — a jacket. A bag. A scar on the wrong arm. The game does not tell you what you're looking at. It just describes what it does.

**Why this works:** The first time the player encounters a Remnant Hollow (cycle 2+), there's already a mechanical possibility that this Hollow was someone they knew this cycle. The Echo makes that possibility real and specific. The player killed that character. The Hollow is what's left. The motor memory is what couldn't be erased.

**Quest mechanics:**
- Cycle 1: The Echo Hollow performs a gesture tied to the player's personal loss. A parent repeatedly folding an empty blanket. A soldier saluting no one. The quest is simply: *observe it long enough to understand it* (a Wits check, DC 8, unlocks "Echo" flag and small Grit gain).
- Cycle 2+: The Echo Hollow is located in or near the player's previous cycle death room. It exhibits a behavior the player performed frequently (combat-based, exploration-based, or skill-based, depending on playstyle). The flag check is silent — the player is never told "this is your old character."

**The resolution:** There is no clean resolution. The player can:
- Kill it (ends the visual loop, gives nothing)
- Leave it (it will still be there; the world pressure system may eventually turn it into a Brute)
- Observe it until the behavior pattern becomes fully legible (triggers a quiet journal entry — a record, not an explanation)

Helping it is not an option. It isn't there anymore. The behavior is not a cry for help. It's an echo.

**On multiple cycles:** The Echo location updates each cycle. If the player always dies in the same general area, the encounters cluster. A player who keeps dying at the River Road checkpoint will eventually find multiple Echoes in that corridor — each doing something different, each recognizable if the player has been paying attention. The corridor can become a gallery of who the player used to be.

**Writing rule:** The Echo Hollow is never described with pity. It's described with precision. What it does, where it stands, what it's holding. The emotional weight comes from the specificity, not from the game telling the player how to feel.

## THE PLAYER'S PERSONAL QUEST

During character creation, the player selects or writes a **personal loss** — someone or something they lost in the Collapse. This becomes a passive quest thread that surfaces unpredictably throughout the game.

**Pre-written options:**

| Loss | What Surfaces |
|------|--------------|
| **A child** (name chosen by player) | You find evidence of your child's path — a name on a refugee list, a drawing on a wall, a description from an NPC who saw a kid matching that description heading toward the mountains. You may find them. They may not be who they were. |
| **A partner** (name chosen by player) | Letters. Sightings. Rumors. Eventually, a trail that leads somewhere you have to decide whether to follow. What if they're Sanguine now? What if they're Hollow? What if they moved on? |
| **A community** (town name chosen by player) | Your hometown. You can eventually travel there (procedurally generated ruins). What you find depends on dice rolls: empty, occupied by Hollow, rebuilt by strangers, or one person still there, waiting. |
| **Your own identity** | You can't remember who you were before the Collapse. Amnesia — viral, traumatic, or chosen. Throughout the game, fragments return: a skill you didn't know you had, a face that recognizes you, a name you don't remember choosing. This path converges with the MERIDIAN storyline in unexpected ways. |
| **A promise** (written by player) | You made a promise to someone — to do something, find something, become something. The game takes the player's written text and weaves references to it into NPC dialogue and found documents. |

These threads never force resolution. The player can pursue them or let them haunt the background. Either is valid. Some threads have satisfying resolutions. Some don't. Some have resolutions that hurt more than not knowing.

## ENDGAME STATES

After the player makes their choice at MERIDIAN, the world changes. The endgame is not a cutscene — it's a transformed game world the player continues to inhabit.

### If the player chose The Cure

- A fragile research effort begins, centered at whatever faction the player aligned with
- Sanguine factions fracture — some cooperate, some go to war to prevent being "cured"
- The first cured Hollow begin appearing. They are traumatized beyond description. They remember.
- New quests involve protecting the research, distributing early doses, and dealing with the political fallout of who gets cured first
- The world is more hopeful. It is also more violent, because hope is something worth killing for.

### If the player chose The Weapon

- The targeted pathogen is deployed. The Hollow die. The Sanguine die or flee.
- The immediate threat is over. The world is quieter.
- Quieter isn't the same as better. The Sanguine who helped you, the Lucid who wanted peace, the Covenant of Dusk — gone.
- The factions turn on each other now that the common enemy is removed. There is nothing like a shared threat to keep people cooperating. Without it, old grudges surface.
- New quests involve managing the aftermath: mass graves, political power vacuums, and the player's own reckoning with what they did.

### If the player chose The Seal

- MERIDIAN is gone. The data is gone. No cure. No weapon. No shortcut.
- The world continues as it is. The Hollow remain. The Sanguine remain. Nothing changes.
- Except something does: the *possibility* of a quick fix is gone, and that forces the factions to deal with each other on real terms. Long-term thinking replaces desperate grabs for a silver bullet.
- New quests involve brokering lasting treaties, building sustainable defenses, and slowly, generation by generation, learning to live in the world as it is.
- This is the quietest ending. It is also, possibly, the wisest.

### If the player chose The Throne

- The player controls MERIDIAN and its contents. Every faction wants what they have.
- The player becomes a faction unto themselves — a new power in the Four Corners.
- Allies become supplicants. Enemies become insurgents. The player must decide, day by day, how to wield power without becoming the thing they fought.
- New quests involve defending MERIDIAN, making impossible allocation decisions (who gets the cure first?), and dealing with assassination attempts, espionage, and the loneliness of the throne.
- This is the ending that asks: *Were you the right person to hold this?*

---

## THE GAME'S FINAL LINE

No matter which ending the player reaches, the game's closing text — delivered by the narrator after the world has changed — ends with the same six words:

> *What's left is what matters.*

It means something different every time.
