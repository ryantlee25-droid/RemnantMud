# Narrative Quality Rubric — The Remnant
_eval-convoy-0503 | P5-A | Created: 2026-05-03_

---

## Purpose

This rubric provides structured scoring criteria for evaluating the quality of narrative prose output generated by The Remnant's five narrative systems:

- **deathProse** — `lib/deathProse.ts` — 9 named variants covering combat, environment, infection, faction, and cycle-aware deaths
- **hollowPressure** — `lib/hollowPressure.ts` — 13 pressure-level narrations (levels 0–10 + clamps)
- **terminalCreation** — `lib/terminalCreation.ts` — character creation flow text
- **terminalDeath** — `lib/terminalDeath.ts` — death screens, the Between, endings, prologue, memory pool
- **narratorVoice** — `lib/narratorVoice.ts` — unreliable narrator whispers
- **playerMonologue** — `lib/playerMonologue.ts` — class-specific internal voice (7 classes × 7 triggers × 5 loss types)

The 125 snapshots in `tests/eval/narrativeSnapshotRegistry.test.ts` form the corpus against which this rubric is applied. Any snapshot updated via `vitest -u` triggers a mandatory manual re-scoring pass before merge.

---

## The Five Dimensions

Score each piece of prose on all five dimensions. Scores are **1–5 integers**. A score of 3 or higher on every dimension is a passing result. Any dimension scoring 1 or 2 is a defect that must be addressed before the prose is considered shippable.

---

### Dimension 1: Voice Specificity

**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses sensory and contextual detail that is grounded in this game's specific world — its geography, its pathogen, its factions, its particular flavor of post-collapse Southwest — rather than generic post-apocalyptic or fantasy language.

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **5** | Uses post-collapse sensory detail unique to this room, zone, or situation. References CHARON-7, the Hollow, the Sanguine, or the Four Corners world by name or implication. The language could not be lifted intact into a generic apocalypse game. |
| **4** | Grounded in world detail. One or two phrases are generic but the overall voice is specific. |
| **3** | Recognizably set in this world but relies on generic post-apocalypse imagery (ruins, silence, darkness, hunger) without distinctive anchor points. Not embarrassing; not distinctive. |
| **2** | Mostly generic. One world-specific reference might appear but feels decorative rather than load-bearing. |
| **1** | Placeholder prose. Could describe a death in any game: "You have died." "You failed." "Your journey ends here." No specificity whatsoever. |

**Common failures**:
- Generic terms ("the creatures," "the infected," "the enemy") instead of the game's specific vocabulary ("the Hollow," "a Shuffler," "the Sanguine")
- Environmental imagery ("the ruins," "the dark") without anchoring to a specific zone
- CHARON-7 or the Revenant mechanic absent from death sequences when they are the defining mechanism of the game

**What to look for in high-scoring prose**:
- CHARON-7 named as the agent of reconstruction
- Zone-specific terms (the Scar's cosmic-horror register, the Pens' political execution register, the River Road's physical exposure register)
- Faction vocabulary used naturally, not explained
- The specific vocabulary of the Revenant mechanic ("reconstruction," "scaffolding," "cycle," "the between")

---

### Dimension 2: Show, Don't Tell

**What it measures**: Whether the prose creates emotional impact through concrete imagery, action, and physical sensation — rather than naming the emotion it wants the player to feel.

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **5** | Every emotional beat is delivered through image, sound, texture, or physical sensation. The word "fear" does not appear; the reader feels it. The word "sad" does not appear; the specificity of loss creates it. |
| **4** | Predominantly shows. One or two instances of direct emotional labeling that feel intentional and controlled rather than lazy. |
| **3** | Mixed approach. Equal amounts of showing and telling. The reader understands the intended emotion but is told it as often as shown it. |
| **2** | Predominantly tells. Emotion is named rather than demonstrated. "You feel afraid." "This was devastating." Imagery exists but is subordinate to declaration. |
| **1** | Tells exclusively. No concrete imagery. A sequence of stated feelings and conclusions without the evidence that would earn them. |

**Common failures**:
- "You feel hopeless" instead of imagery that creates hopelessness
- "This is terrifying" instead of a specific thing that is terrifying
- Summarizing what the player should take away ("This loss will stay with you") instead of presenting the moment that makes it stay

**What to look for in high-scoring prose**:
- Physical sensation tied to the death mechanism (the cold specificity of the infection, the ground that "didn't need to try")
- Action without narrated emotion (the Hollow "moved on" after killing you — not "indifferently moved on")
- Details that imply rather than state (the faction kept "your name on a list" — implies threat without naming it)

---

### Dimension 3: Class Differentiation

**What it measures**: For playerMonologue output, whether the character class is perceptible in the prose's word choice, register, and internal logic. For shared prose systems (deathProse, narratorVoice), whether the text respects class-neutral language that does not accidentally skew toward a single class archetype.

**Note**: deathProse and narratorVoice are class-neutral by design. For those systems, score 3 by default unless the prose leans unmistakably toward one class archetype (score 2) or explicitly stereotypes a class (score 1). The full 1–5 range applies to playerMonologue only.

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **5** | (playerMonologue only) The player's class is immediately perceptible from word choice and internal logic alone, without reading the class name. An Enforcer's survival calculus reads differently from a Broker's social calculus and a Wraith's isolation calculus. Pools for different classes are not interchangeable. |
| **4** | (playerMonologue) Class flavor is present and consistent. A reader familiar with the seven classes would identify it correctly. One or two lines could belong to another class without breaking the overall voice. |
| **3** | (playerMonologue) Class flavor present but subtle. Correct identification requires knowing the class. For class-neutral systems: the expected score when prose is genuinely universal. |
| **2** | (playerMonologue) Class voice is generic enough to fit two or more classes. For class-neutral systems: prose has drifted toward one class's register. |
| **1** | (playerMonologue) No distinguishable class voice. Identical to any other class. For class-neutral systems: prose explicitly stereotypes or names a class archetype it should not. |

**The seven class registers** (reference for evaluators):

| Class | Internal Voice Register |
|-------|------------------------|
| Enforcer | Tactical, economy-of-force, treats the body as a tool, low-affect |
| Scout | Observational, spatial, notices what others miss, cautious optimism |
| Wraith | Isolated, hypervigilant, trust deficit, precise and slightly paranoid |
| Shepherd | Communal, grief-forward, responsible for others even when alone |
| Reclaimer | Technical, systematic, frames problems as solvable systems |
| Warden | Patient, long-horizon, environmental attunement, stoic |
| Broker | Transactional, strategic, reads social geometry, never fully relaxed |

---

### Dimension 4: Pacing and Rhythm

**What it measures**: Whether sentences vary in length, whether the rhythm of the prose matches its emotional content, and whether the writing creates a readable cadence rather than a wall of uniform text.

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **5** | Deliberate variation in sentence length, used to mirror emotional content. Short sentences at high-tension moments. Longer sentences for contemplative or atmospheric beats. Rhythm reads as intentional. The prose has musical structure. |
| **4** | Good variation. Two or three moments where the rhythm is not optimized, but overall pacing serves the content. |
| **3** | Uniform sentence length (all medium-long or all short) but not distractingly so. The prose is readable but does not use rhythm as a tool. |
| **2** | Noticeable pacing problem: a wall of long sentences that flattens emotional peaks, or a staccato burst of short sentences that creates confusion rather than emphasis. |
| **1** | Single-sentence output, or prose where every sentence is identical in structure (all begin "You...", all end with a period at the same length). No rhythm whatsoever. |

**Common failures**:
- Death sequences that are entirely long, even-length sentences — the sameness dilutes impact at the climax
- Emotional peaks (the moment of death, the reconstruction, the faction verdict) buried in the middle of long sentences rather than given room to land
- Missing the short punching sentence that ends a sequence

**What to look for in high-scoring prose**:
- The isolated short sentence that terminates a thought and lets silence follow ("It wasn't." — combat_veteran variant)
- Variable clause density: sometimes front-loaded, sometimes end-weighted
- Fragments used intentionally at emotional peaks, not accidentally

---

### Dimension 5: Clean of Verbal Tics

**What it measures**: Whether the prose avoids the characteristic tells of bad LLM-generated or rushed human writing: filler adverbs, tautological openers, redundant hedging, and the specific verbal tics called out in The Remnant's narrative bible and house style.

**Banned terms and patterns** (any appearance is a defect):
- `specific [adjective]` constructions ("the specific texture," "the specific quality of darkness") — acceptable once per piece; three or more occurrences is a tic
- `methodically` — house-banned adverb
- Tautological openers: "It was a thing that...", "What it was, was...", "The fact that..."
- Certainty hedges: "in a way," "somehow," "sort of," "almost as if" — acceptable once per piece if earned
- Passive-voice pileup: three or more consecutive passive constructions
- Exclamatory tell-don't-show: "Incredible." "Devastating." "Terrifying." as standalone sentences
- Redundant death labels: "You are dead." "This is your death." as the lead line (acceptable as callback if earned)

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **5** | Zero instances of banned terms. No verbal tics. Every word is load-bearing. The prose is clean. |
| **4** | One instance of a banned term or near-tic. Does not damage the overall quality. |
| **3** | Two instances, or one that is distracting. Reader notices but is not ejected from the experience. |
| **2** | Three or more instances, or one egregious example that dominates the prose (the banned term appears in a climactic sentence). |
| **1** | Multiple banned terms or tics distributed throughout. The prose reads as formulaic or procedurally generated rather than crafted. |

**The "specific" pattern specifically**: The word "specific" used as an intensifier ("the specific texture," "the specific weight") is a conscious stylistic choice in this codebase (see `combat_veteran` and `environment_collapse` variants). It is permitted up to once per prose piece. Two or more occurrences in a single variant = score 4 on this dimension. Three or more = score 3 or below.

## Worked Example

### Variant: `combat_overwhelmed`
**Source**: `lib/deathProse.ts` lines 102–109  
**Context**: `{ cause: 'combat', cycle: 1, hollowKills: 2 }`  
**Snapshot**: `narrativeSnapshotRegistry.test.ts` — `deathProse — DEATH_PROSE_VARIANTS > variant: combat_overwhelmed (cycle 1, <5 kills)`

**Prose text**:

> You wanted more time. The Hollow didn't ask.
>
> It wasn't supposed to go like this. You had a pack. You had a plan. You had the specific weight of intention that feels like readiness until the moment it isn't.
>
> CHARON-7 is not surprised. It has been through this before. It will reconstruct something that can pass for you. It will be a little smaller than you were.

---

**Scoring**:

**Dimension 1 — Voice Specificity: 5**

The prose is irreducibly specific to this game. "The Hollow" is named by the game-specific term rather than "the enemy." CHARON-7 is named as the mechanism of reconstruction — a concept that only exists in this game's cosmology. "Reconstruct something that can pass for you" activates the Revenant mechanic's specific horror: you are not restored, you are replaced. "It will be a little smaller than you were" has no parallel in generic games. None of this text could be transplanted to another setting without rewriting every sentence.

**Dimension 2 — Show, Don't Tell: 5**

The devastation of being a first-timer who prepared and died anyway is never stated. It is shown through the three-beat list: "You had a pack. You had a plan. You had the specific weight of intention that feels like readiness until the moment it isn't." The word "disappointed" does not appear. The word "unprepared" does not appear. The gap between readiness and its failure is presented through the structure of the sentence itself — accumulation that dissolves. "It will be a little smaller than you were" creates dread without using the word dread.

**Dimension 3 — Class Differentiation: 3 (expected)**

Death prose is class-neutral by design. This variant scores 3 by default. The language is universal — "a pack," "a plan," "intention" — none of it anchors to any particular class. Appropriate.

**Dimension 4 — Pacing and Rhythm: 5**

Opening two sentences: one five-word, one four-word. High punch, low word count. Paragraph two builds with accumulation — three parallel "You had" clauses — then cuts with a subordinate clause that subverts the setup. Paragraph three shifts to declarative machinery: three sentences of approximately equal, short length, the CHARON-7 voice cold and factual. The switch from the player's voice register (accumulating, expectant) to CHARON-7's register (cold, clinical, past-tense of future events) is done entirely through rhythm. The final sentence lands because it is the shortest in the piece.

**Dimension 5 — Clean of Verbal Tics: 4**

One occurrence of the "specific [adjective]" pattern: "the specific weight of intention." This is the single permitted use. No banned adverbs. No tautological openers. No passive pileup. One permitted use of the stylistic move that is the codebase's characteristic intensifier. Score 4 rather than 5 because of this occurrence; would be 5 if the phrase were absent or this were a piece that hadn't used its one allocation.

**TOTAL: 22 / 25 | PASS**

**Evaluator notes**: The "specific weight of intention" construction is the single most visible verbal tic risk in this codebase. It is used well here — once, at an emotionally appropriate moment, and it carries meaning. The risk is accumulation across variants. A reviewer running this rubric against the full corpus should flag any variant that uses "specific [adjective]" more than once internally, and separately track the cross-variant rate.

## How to Use This Rubric

### Manual Workflow

1. **Pull the snapshot file** — open `tests/eval/narrativeSnapshotRegistry.test.ts`. The inline snapshot values are the canonical prose text for each test case.

2. **Run the test suite** to confirm the snapshot file is current and no snapshots are stale:
   ```bash
   pnpm test:eval
   ```
   If any snapshot is stale (marked as obsolete), update with `vitest -u` and do not score until the snapshots reflect the committed codebase.

3. **Select your sample** — use the 20-sample set above for baseline, or select a custom set from the 125 snapshots if targeting a specific module.

4. **Score each piece** using the five dimension definitions. Fill out a scoring sheet per variant. Do not score multiple variants in sequence without breaks — fatigue creates scoring drift.

5. **Record results** in `evaluation/narrative-review/narrative-quality-scores.md`.

6. **Flag defects** immediately — any variant scoring 1 or 2 on any dimension is a defect requiring authorial attention, not a test failure. Open an issue or PR comment.

7. **Re-score after prose edits** — any PR that updates prose files (`lib/deathProse.ts`, `lib/narratorVoice.ts`, `lib/playerMonologue.ts`, `lib/terminalDeath.ts`, `lib/terminalCreation.ts`, `lib/hollowPressure.ts`, or any file in `data/narratorVoices.ts` / `data/playerMonologues/`) must include a rubric re-score of the affected variants before the PR is approved.

### Trigger: When to Score

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Initial baseline (first run) | Score all 20 samples in the recommended set |
| Any prose file edit in a PR | Score all affected variants (minimum 5) |
| `vitest -u` snapshot update | Score all updated variants before merge |
| New death variant added | Score the new variant against all 5 dimensions |
| New player class added | Score all 7 triggers × the new class (minimum 7 variants) |
| Narrative bible update (voice spec change) | Full 20-sample re-score against updated spec |

### LLM-as-Judge Wiring Notes

This rubric is designed to be human-applied but is structured for eventual LLM-judge automation. The following notes are for a future implementation:

**Dimension formulation**: Each dimension has explicit anchors at 1, 3, and 5 with named failure modes. An LLM judge should be given the dimension definition plus the banned-terms list (Dimension 5) as a structured prompt, not asked to "assess quality" in the abstract.

**Scoring prompt structure** (recommended):
```
You are scoring narrative prose from a post-apocalyptic text game against a quality rubric.
Score the following prose on [DIMENSION NAME] on a scale of 1–5, where:
- 5: [anchor text]
- 3: [anchor text]
- 1: [anchor text]

Common failures to penalize: [list]

Prose:
"""
[prose text]
"""

Return a JSON object: {"score": <integer 1-5>, "justification": "<one sentence>"}
```

**Dimension ordering**: Dimensions 1, 2, and 5 are most reliably automated (they have lexical and structural signals). Dimensions 3 and 4 require more contextual judgment. Run dimensions 1, 2, 5 first; use them as a filter before applying the more expensive 3 and 4 evaluations.

**Calibration**: Before automated scoring, run the judge against the worked example in this document and verify it reaches the same scores (1: 5, 2: 5, 3: 3, 4: 5, 5: 4). If it disagrees by more than 1 point on any dimension, recalibrate the prompt before trusting automated results.

**Snapshot integration**: The snapshot test suite catches prose drift. The LLM judge catches prose quality regression. They are complementary and must both pass for a prose PR to be considered clean.

**Rate limiting**: At 125 snapshots × 5 dimensions, an LLM judge pass requires 625 API calls minimum. Batch or parallelize per the API's batch endpoint. Do not run the judge in CI by default — it is a pre-release gate, not a per-commit gate.
